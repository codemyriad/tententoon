ten·ten·toon /ˌtɛn.tɛnˈtoːn/ noun

1. a self-repeating image whose copies spiral as they shrink, in the manner of M. C. Escher's Print Gallery.

• a tententoon of the old gallery, winding inward without end.

Origin coined from the Dutch Prentententoonstelling (“print exhibition”): prenten, prints + tentoonstelling, exhibition. The coined word sits right where the two halves meet.

Start with something you've seen

Put a picture inside itself. Then put it inside that copy, and inside the next, and don't stop.

Every copy sits squarely inside the one before it. The picture drops straight down into itself, shrinking by the same step each time, forever. That is the Droste effect, and you've seen it a hundred times: on the cocoa tin, in two facing mirrors, anywhere a thing holds a smaller echo of itself.

Now bend it

Here's a stranger question. What if each copy doesn't only shrink — what if it also turns?

Below is the same idea, a copy inside a copy inside a copy, except now every copy is rotated a little as it shrinks. The whole stack winds up into a spiral. Drag the twist from a flat Droste fall up to the angle where the spiral seals shut.

At 0° it's the plain Droste, dropping straight in. Nudge the twist up and watch the edges: straight lines bow into curves, the room leans over, and still nothing tears. There is one special angle — the one marked closed ✓ — where every line, followed inward, comes back to precisely where it began. Try snap it shut to land on it exactly.

How can a spiral be “straight”?

That spiral looks impossibly complicated: curved, tightening, endless. Here is the secret that tames it, and it needs no mathematics at all.

Picture a spiral staircase seen from straight above. It coils inward and seems to fall away forever. But walk down it, and chart your height against the distance you've walked. That chart is a plain, straight ramp. Same staircase — one version rolled up, the other laid flat.

Unroll the spiral yourself

So let's do exactly that to the spiral above: unroll it. Slide the control, or press play, and watch the tightening coil straighten into a flat, repeating strip.

Laid flat, the mystery is gone. There's no centre to fall into and no curve to tear — just one tile, copied over and over without end. That flattening move has a name you may remember from school: the logarithm. It measures things not by how big but by how many times you've multiplied — so shrinking by the same step, again and again, turns into plain, even spacing. Every spiral in this whole story is just a strip, rolled up.

Slide the strip, spin the picture

Now that it's flat, play with it. The panel on the left is the unrolled strip itself; mathematicians write it log(z − c). Drag it.

drag the log: ↔ zooms the picture, ↕ spins it

This live experiment needs WebGL2 and JavaScript. In short: a sideways slide in the flat log strip zooms the picture, and an up-and-down slide spins it.

A sideways drag zooms the picture; an up-and-down drag spins it. There's the whole trick in one sentence: a plain slide in the flat world is a zoom-and-turn in the round one. Slide by exactly one tile and the picture lands back on itself, identical. Roll the strip up again, and “slide by one tile” becomes “go once around the spiral.” The loop closes because the slide closes. The seam isn't hidden; there simply isn't one.

For the curious: the actual map

Everything above is a single idea with the arithmetic filled in. Measure each point by its distance from the centre c and its angle; take the logarithm of the distance. The endless nested frames flatten into a rectangular lattice — step sideways by log S for one Droste jump, up by 2π for one full turn around c.

A copy that shrinks and turns leans that lattice over by a fixed angle, β = arctan(log S / 2π): the one tilt that lets the pattern line back up with itself after a slide. (Drag the twist near the top to closed ✓ and you have set exactly this β; the unroll panel is that same leaned-over lattice.) Roll the logarithm back up and the tilted lattice winds into the spiral, caught in a single line:

w(z) = c + (z − c)α,  α = 1 − i·(log S / 2π).

The real part carries the picture; the imaginary part is the lean. That — all of it — is the tententoon.

Escher got there first, by hand

Escher had no computer in 1956. He worked the curved grid out by eye, ruled it onto the canvas, and painted a gallery, a print, a town, and the gallery again into the bend. And he got it right: the mathematics later showed his intuition was very nearly exact.

But a spiral tightens forever toward its centre, and a pen can only go so fine. So Escher stopped, left a soft white patch in the middle of the picture, curled his signature into it, and called it finished. The one place the picture could not finish itself.

The map that filled the hole

In 2003, two mathematicians in Leiden (Bart de Smit and Hendrik Lenstra) worked out the exact map hiding in Escher's grid. The idealised Print Gallery, they showed, contains a complete copy of itself rotated by 157.6256° and shrunk by a factor of 22.5837. Pin those two numbers down and the rest of the picture is forced, including the part Escher left blank.

This is not a theorem so much as a recipe: with the map in hand, you can hand the missing centre to a computer and have it do what a pen could not. That is exactly what they did, continuing the spiral inward far past the reach of any hand and closing the white hole at last.
